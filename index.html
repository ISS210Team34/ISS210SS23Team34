About

A learning disability is a neurological disorder that affects one’s ability to process and understand information. This can affect someone’s ability to read, write, listen, spell. memorize, and/or speak. Learning disabilities can vary in severity and may affect one skill or multiple. Normally, learning disabilities will show up or be detectable form birth to early childhood and, depending on the person, may require accommodations or the person may be able to overcome their disability. Facts: Research has found that 8-10% of American children under the age of 18 have some type of learning disability 2.3 million students have been diagnosed with specific learning disabilities and receive services under IDEA; this makes up 35% of all the students who are receiving special education services 75-85% of special education students identified as LD have basic deficits in reading and language 60% of adults with severe literacy problems are undiagnosed/untreated for their disability

Most Common Learning Disabilities

Most Common Learning Disabilities: Dyslexia is a condition of neurodevelopmental origin that mainly affects someone's ability to read, write, and spell. It occurs in children with normal vision and intelligence. Treatment can help this condition but there is no cure. Symptoms include reversing the position of letters, difficulty with the ability to break down words into their constituent parts, struggle with reading comprehension, delayed speech, and difficulty learning auditory processing disorder new vocabulary or rhymes. Most children suffering can succeed in school with tutoring or a specialized education program. ADHD, or Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder, is a neurological disorder that affects a person’s ability to pay attention, control impulsivity, and regulate their level of hyperactivity. This is the second most common learning disability and can affect people of all ages, though most commonly diagnosed in children. Symptoms include inability to sit still, being disorganized or forgetful, difficulty staying quiet and attentive, lack of motivation, and mood swings or emotional outbursts. Dyscaculia is a learning disability that affects math skills. It can make it difficult for someone to understand math concepts, perform arithmetic calculations, and solve math problems. Lots of people dislike math, but they’re eventually able to understand it with enough practice. Dyscalculia is more severe because it’s diagnosing a learning disability that makes it challenging to understand even the most basic math concepts. Symptoms include struggling with mathematical concepts such as measuring, time, and estimating, being unsure of how to do basic math problems, difficulty following the order of operations, and finding it challenging to count and group numbers together.

Who can get learning disabilites?

Learning disabilities can develop in people of all ages, races, and/or socioeconomic backgrounds. Anyone can be affected and it is usually diagnosed from birth to early childhood. However, there are many learning disabilities that have been found to be inherited therefore making certain people more susceptible to possibly inheriting different learning disabilities. Also, certain factors may increase the risk of developing a learning disability such as premature birth, lighter birth weight, and exposure to toxins or infection during pregnancy.

Accomodations given to children with learning disabilities

Sometimes in education, disabilities are diagnosed when not needed to provide accommodations. The threshold of disability is not very clearly defined, so very often children with certain academic weaknesses are given accommodations, such as extra time on standardized tests that is not necessary. This creates an unfair advantage for children who get tested for learning disabilities, which are very often misdiagnosed. This led to a large discrepancy in scores on the SAT and ACT tests. In more wealthy, well off areas, children are much more likely to be diagnosed with “invisible” learning disabilities that affect them cognitively. Both the children that are well off and struggling financially have the same disabilities, but lower-class students receive accommodation much less often.

How to involve disabled children socially

One main way to help students with disabilities to feel comfortable with learning in a school setting is to make them feel like they belong. A great way to do this is by building the social aspect of their experience in a school day. This can be done by creating playgrounds where people with and without disabilities can play together. However, as the article states, putting both disabled and non-disabled children on a playground together will not guarantee that they play together. So, children who are not disabled should be taught to not have certain biases towards disabled children. They should also be taught about inclusion to try and break social barriers.

How education reform would help children with disabilities

Everyone deserves the right to a fair, free education. The first reform of education for disabled individuals was in 1975, with the Disabilities Education Act passed by President Ford in 1975. This act guaranteed the right to a free and appropriate education for people with disabilities. While this was an important step in progressing disabled rights, it hasn’t been the reality for many students. To this day, there are still a high number of disabled students that are unable to read on a grade level, or graduate college. There are ways to improve the quality of education in ways that will make a difference. Disability in education reform starts with educating students without disabilities. Students with and without disabilities should be educated in the same classroom. Disabled students deserve the same free education, in the least restrictive environment. It is important to provide multiple methods of representation in learning. For example, providing options for perception, language/symbols, and comprehension. All of these things can help create a better future for disability in education.

How assistive technology can help children with disabilities

With the use of technologies such as type assist, and interactive screens to help children with learning disabilities be able to more clearly understand the content and concepts of the classroom, a huge step forward is taken in combating the struggles of learning disabilities. Displaying the information in a way that makes more sense to a student who struggles to grasp concepts “normally” some would say makes a huge difference in their learning. This is a perfect example of how everyone learns differently, and just because they struggle, doesn't mean they are incapable of learning. By providing assistive technologies to the classroom, students with certain learning abilities can be provided with assistance in any of the 5 senses in order to encourage their learning. Not only do these assistive technologies help integrate students with learning disabilities into a regular classroom, studies have shown that students who had access, and used assistive technologies throughout school performed better and were more well adjusted to life beyond the classroom.
